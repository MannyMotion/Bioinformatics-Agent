RNA degradation…..

Ribonucleases (RNases) degrade RNA by catalysing the cleavage of phosphodiester bonds between nucleotides

Classes of RNases:

1. Exoribonucleases – These degrade RNA by removing nucleotides one at a time from either the 5' or 3' end.


5' → 3' exoribonucleases (e.g., Xrn1) start degradation at the 5' end after decapping.


3' → 5' exoribonucleases (e.g., the exosome complex) degrade RNA from the 3' end.

RNA degradation…..

Classes of RNases:


2. Endoribonucleases – These cleave RNA at internal sites, generating fragments that can be further degraded by exoribonucleases.

Examples include
RNase A- which cleaves after pyrimidines (C, U)
RNase III- which recognises double-stranded RNA
RNase T1- Cleaves after guanine residues

RNA degradation…..

3. RNases with Specific Functions:


RNase P – Processes precursor tRNA by cleaving its 5' leader sequence.


RNase H – Degrades RNA in RNA-DNA hybrids, important for removing Okazaki fragments in DNA replication.


RNase L – Degrades viral and cellular RNA in response to interferon signalling.


Degradation can occur in both normal RNA turnover (e.g., mRNA decay) and in response to stress or infection.

Complementary DNA (cDNA) Synthesis

Crucial molecular biological technique used to create a DNA copy of RNA 

Reverse Transcription:

An oligo(dT) fragment is used as a primer at the 3’ Poly A tail

Reverse transcriptase used the RNA as a template to synthesis cDNA by adding dNTPs

This results in first strand synthesis

High temperatures are then used to separate the RNA and cDNA strand (single-stranded cDNA is used for some downstream applications)

A specific primer can anneal to the cDNA and Taq polymerase will add nucleotides to form ds-cDNA

Quantitative PCR

Powerful molecular biology technique used to amplify and quantify nucleic acids

Monitors DNA amplification in real-time using fluorescence

Principles of qPCR:
Denaturation (95°C): DNA strands separate.
Annealing (50-65°C): Primers bind to the target DNA.
Extension (72°C): DNA polymerase synthesises new DNA strands.



fluorescent dye or probe is used to monitor DNA amplification after each cycle.
The fluorescence intensity correlates with the amount of amplified DNA.

Fluorescence Detection Methods


Two main strategies are used to detect DNA amplification in qPCR:


SYBR Green Dye-Based qPCR

SYBR Green is a fluorescent dye that binds to double-stranded DNA (dsDNA).
Fluorescence increases as more dsDNA is produced.
Advantages: Simple and cost-effective.
Disadvantages: Non-specific binding can lead to false signals.


2. Probe-Based qPCR (TaqMan Probes)

Uses a fluorescently labelled probe (e.g., TaqMan) that binds to the target DNA sequence.
The 5′ nuclease activity of Taq polymerase cleaves the probe during amplification, releasing fluorescence.
Advantages: High specificity and multiplexing capability.
Disadvantages: More expensive than SYBR Green.

Types of qPCR Analysis

Absolute Quantification:
Measures exact DNA copy numbers using a standard curve.
Used in viral load testing, bacterial quantification, and plasmid copy number analysis.


Relative Quantification:
Compares gene expression levels relative to a reference gene.
Common in gene expression studies (e.g., cancer research, drug response).

Key qPCR Parameters


Relative quantification/ Livak method

Threshold Cycle (Ct or Cq): The cycle number at which fluorescence exceeds the background level.
Lower Cq → Higher initial DNA concentration.
Higher Cq → Lower initial DNA concentration.

2. ΔCq Method: Used for relative quantification:
ΔCq= Cq target− Cq reference

3. Δ Δ Cq Method: Used for normalisation against reference gene:
Δ Δ Cq= Cq Sample− Cq control

Expression changes are calculated using fold change (2^(-ΔΔCt)).
