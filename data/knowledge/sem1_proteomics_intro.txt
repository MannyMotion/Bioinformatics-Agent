To make new cells, an existing cell divides in two. But first it copies its DNA so the new cells will each have a complete set of genetic instructions. Cells sometimes make mistakes during the copying process - kind of like typos. These typos lead to variations in the DNA sequence at particular locations, called single nucleotide polymorphisms, or SNPs (pronounced "snips").

SNPs can generate biological variation between people by causing differences in the recipes for proteins that are written in genes. Those differences can in turn influence a variety of traits such as appearance, disease susceptibility or response to drugs. While some SNPs lead to differences in health or physical appearance, most SNPs seem to lead to no observable differences between people at all.

DNA is passed from parent to child, so you inherit your SNPs versions from your parents. You will be a match with your siblings, grandparents, aunts, uncles, and cousins at many of these SNPs. But you will have far fewer matches with people to whom you are only distantly related. The number of SNPs where you match another person can therefore be used to tell how closely related you are.

Examples – ICDH, gifitinib kinase target, straight hair, curly hair, eye colour

In proteomic the key to fingerprinting is to degrade the proteins in to peptides using specific proteases that act in an analogous manner to restriction endonucleases. 

Even though some 20 products can be produced by digesting this protein with trypsin, in practice we only detect and only need to detect a small number of the peptides to determine of the protein is present in a sample. Some such as single, amino acids, di- and tri-peptides, we will not see as they are too small, and sometimes rarely, they can be too big. Trypsin digestion, in addition to being reliable and having relatively simple cleavage rules, also has the advantage of digesting at two amino acids that are relatively uncommon (lys, arg 5.7% each), yielding many peptides of a size ideally suitable for mass spectrometry – 700 and 3500Da. Chymotrypsin which cleaves at more amino acids of medium to higher frequency (F, Y, W, L, I, M – 3.9, 3.2, 1.3, 9.0. 5.2, 2.4%), produce peptides that are too small, whereas cyanogen bromide that cleaves only at methionine (2.4%) a relatively low abundance amino acid produces peptides that are frequently too big.

Mass spectrometry is important for peptide analysis because of the constituent amino acids. Amino acids can be split into groups based on physical and chemical properties such as hydrophobicity or acidity. Every amino acid has a valuable physicochemical function that it contributes towards the function of each protein. E.g. Prolines – Dr Jones lectures – protein folding.
